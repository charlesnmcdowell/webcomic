# WEBTOON STRUCTURED NARRATIVE FRAMEWORK
## Story Constitution v2.1
### Based on: Solo Leveling, Tower of God, Chainsaw Man analysis
### Updated with: Hollow Zounds development lessons

---

## CORE PHILOSOPHY

A good webtoon story follows rules the reader never sees.
The reader only feels the result — they're hooked, they understand,
they care. These rules exist to produce that result consistently
without manual intervention.

Every story beat must pass these rules before it becomes panels.
No exceptions.

---

## SECTION 1 — WORLD RULES

### Rule 1: 3-Panel World Establishment
The reader must understand the world within 3 panels.
No more. No less.

Required information in those 3 panels:
- What is the world's central conflict or abnormality?
- What do people do about it?
- What is the cost of that thing?

**PASS:** "Void Holes open. Creatures come out. Anoms fight them."
**FAIL:** Long exposition about history, economics,
rankings, political structures — save that for later.

### Rule 2: Show World Through Civilian Behavior
Never explain the world through narration alone.
Show how NORMAL PEOPLE react to it.

Normal people reacting casually = this world has been like this a while.
Normal people reacting with fear = this is new and dangerous.
Their behavior tells the reader everything without a word of exposition.

### Rule 3: Action Before Backstory
The reader must SEE the world in action before they learn
about the protagonist. At least one action scene or world
event must happen before the main character is introduced.

---

## SECTION 2 — CHARACTER INTRODUCTION RULES

### Rule 4: Two Sentence Character Introduction
The protagonist's introduction must be completable
in two sentences maximum.

Format:
Sentence 1 = Name + Role
Sentence 2 = Core problem or defining trait

**PASS:** "My name is Hollow Zounds. E-rank. Basically a regular person with paperwork."
**FAIL:** Three panels of monologue about the character's name,
hobbies, app, sensei, and personal history before anything happens.

### Rule 5: Stakes Must Be Personal and Visible
The reader must understand what the protagonist stands to
LOSE within the first chapter. Not told. Shown.

Ask: What happens if the protagonist fails?
That answer must be visible on the page — not narrated.

**PASS:** Show bills on the table. No explanation needed.
**FAIL:** Character tells us their motivation in a text panel.

### Rule 6: Weakness Before Power
The protagonist's limitation must be established
BEFORE their power or ability is shown.

The reader must feel the gap between where the character is
and where they need to be. That gap creates investment.

**PASS:** Hollow shown as E-rank, untrained, no income —
THEN the Void Hole incident happens.
**FAIL:** Character is immediately shown as special or different
before we understand what normal looks like.

### Rule 7: One Clear Want
The protagonist must have exactly ONE clear want
that the reader can state in a single sentence.

That want must be:
- Personal (affects them directly)
- Visible (shown not told)
- Relatable (reader understands why they want it)
- Ironic (the way they pursue it should be funny or tragic)

**PASS:** Hollow needs money. Bills visible. Grandma sick.
**FAIL:** Character wants to "get stronger" with no personal reason.

---

## SECTION 3 — SCENE RULES

### Rule 8: Every Scene Has One Job
Every scene must do exactly one of these things:

1. SETUP — introduces information the reader will need later
2. PAYOFF — delivers on something set up earlier
3. ESCALATION — raises the stakes of an existing conflict
4. CHARACTER REVEAL — shows who the protagonist truly is
   under pressure

If a scene doesn't do one of these four things — cut it.

### Rule 9: The Scene Logic Test
Before writing any scene ask:
- Why is this scene happening NOW?
- What does the protagonist WANT in this scene?
- What is STOPPING them from getting it?
- How does this scene end DIFFERENTLY than it started?

If you can't answer all four — the scene isn't ready.

### Rule 10: Comedy Comes From Character Logic
A joke only works if it comes from who the character IS.

The setup is the character's established trait.
The punchline is that trait applied to a new situation.

**PASS:** Hollow's posture is relaxed while Anoms around
him are terrified. Comedy from contrast. No props needed.

**FAIL:** Random prop comedy (sandwich) before the reader
knows the character well enough to find it funny.

## SECTION 4 — PACING RULES

### Rule 11: The 3-Beat Panel Rule
Every sequence of panels must follow this rhythm:

Beat 1 — ESTABLISH (show the situation)
Beat 2 — COMPLICATE (something unexpected happens)
Beat 3 — REACT (character response reveals character)

This applies to action sequences, comedy sequences,
and dramatic sequences equally.

### Rule 12: Text Volume Rule
Text on any single panel must be readable in 3 seconds.
If it takes longer — cut it in half.

Maximum text per panel type:
- Action panel: 1 short narration box OR 1 dialogue bubble
- Establishing panel: 2 narration boxes maximum
- Character close-up: 1 thought OR 1 line of dialogue
- Text-only panel: 4 lines maximum, punchline on final line

### Rule 13: The Silence Rule
At least 20% of panels must have NO text.
Let the art carry the story.
If every panel has text the reader never rests.
Silence creates rhythm. Rhythm creates pace.

---

## SECTION 5 — CHAPTER STRUCTURE RULES

### Rule 14: Chapter Opening Formula
Panel 1-3: World or situation established visually
Panel 4-6: Protagonist introduced or reestablished
Panel 7+: Conflict or complication begins

### Rule 15: Chapter Closing Formula
Every chapter must end on ONE of these:
1. A question the reader needs answered
2. An emotional beat that recontextualizes what happened
3. A threat or revelation that raises stakes
4. A comedy beat that plants a seed for later

**NEVER end on:** Pure cool visual with no narrative hook.
The reader needs a REASON to scroll to the next chapter.

### Rule 16: The Callback Rule
Every chapter must SET UP at least one thing
that pays off in a LATER chapter.

And every chapter must PAY OFF at least one thing
set up in an EARLIER chapter.

This creates the feeling that the story is going somewhere.

## SECTION 6 — THE LOGIC CHECKLIST

Run every scene through this before generating panels.

**CHARACTER LOGIC:**
- [ ] Does the character's action match their established personality?
- [ ] Does the character have a clear reason for being in this scene?
- [ ] Would this character actually say/do this?
- [ ] Is the character's motivation selfish or survival based? (Rule 20)

**WORLD LOGIC:**
- [ ] Does this event make sense in the established world?
- [ ] Is there a realistic reason these two characters are together?
- [ ] Does this location make sense for these characters?
- [ ] Does the timeline make sense? (Rule 21)

**STORY LOGIC:**
- [ ] Does this scene advance the plot OR reveal character?
- [ ] Has everything introduced in this scene been set up before?
- [ ] Does this scene create a new question OR answer an old one?
- [ ] Is information being revealed at the right time? (Rule 17)

**COMEDY LOGIC:**
- [ ] Does this joke come from character or situation?
- [ ] Is the setup clear before the punchline lands?
- [ ] Does the joke make the character MORE themselves or less?
- [ ] Is the joke grounded in world logic? (Rule 18)
- [ ] Has the reader been hooked by action before this joke? (Rule 22)

**ECONOMY/SYSTEMS LOGIC:**
- [ ] Has the relevant system been established before stakes depend on it? (Rule 19)
- [ ] Does the character's financial situation make sense in the world?
- [ ] Is the economic motivation visible not narrated?

**SLICE OF LIFE CHECK:**
- [ ] Has the reader been hooked by action first? (Rule 22)
- [ ] If not — is this scene action, world building, or stakes? If none — cut it.

## SECTION 7 — THE HOLLOW ZOUNDS SPECIFIC RULES

### HZ Rule 1: Hollow Never Knows
Hollow must NEVER understand why something worked.
His explanation is always wrong and always confident.
The reader always knows the real reason.
That gap IS the comedy.

### HZ Rule 2: Luck Is Invisible
The luck stat never announces itself.
It works through coincidence, environment, and physics.
Never through magic beams or obvious supernatural effects.
The more mundane the mechanism — the funnier the result.

### HZ Rule 3: Running Gag After Hook
The "never gets lucky with girls" running gag does NOT
appear until Chapter 2 or later.
The reader must be hooked by action first.
Earn the slice of life. Don't front load it.

### HZ Rule 4: Grandma Is The Anchor
Whenever the story gets too fantastical or high stakes —
cut to grandma. She is normal. She is grounded.
BUT — grandma scenes only appear AFTER the action hook.
Do not introduce grandma as comedy relief before Chapter 1.

### HZ Rule 5: The World Takes Hollow Seriously
Everyone around Hollow treats him as a serious threat
or mysterious anomaly. Faction agents, officials, Anoms —
they all think he's playing 4D chess.
He is not.
That contrast must be maintained in every chapter.

### HZ Rule 6: Motivation Revealed Gradually
The luck stat, the grinding, the "getting lucky" motivation —
none of this is explained in Chapter 0.
Chapter 0 plants the mystery.
Chapter 1 begins to reveal it.
The full picture emerges over multiple chapters.

### HZ Rule 7: Stat Reveals Are Chapter 1 Territory
The luck stat ERROR, the app, the scanner readings —
none of these are explained in Chapter 0.
They are mysteries that pull the reader into Chapter 1.
Chapter 0 only shows the EFFECT — not the cause.

### Rule 23: Character Presence Justification
Every character appearing in a scene must have a logical
reason for being there that is established BEFORE or DURING
the scene.

Ask: Why is this character in this location at this time?
If you cannot answer in one sentence — they should not be
in the scene.

**PASS:** Faction response team arrives at the Void Hole
because that is their job. They got pulled in while responding.
**FAIL:** Fully geared professional Anoms randomly standing
near a surprise Void Hole at midnight with no explanation.

## RECURRING CHARACTER — DARIUS LOWE

**Role:** B-rank Anom. Faction response team member.
The sole survivor of the Void Hole incident with Hollow.

**Visual design (lock these for consistency):**
- Black male, early 30s, athletic but not bulky
- Short fade, clean cut
- Short beard, well maintained
- Light practical armor — armored vest over dark tactical clothing
- Short sword or combat blade on his hip
- **Scar above his left eyebrow — primary recognition detail**
- Expression default: focused, tired, professional

**Character details:**
- Three kids at home
- Been doing this long enough to be competent, not long enough to be rich
- Survives the Void Hole incident with Hollow
- Cannot explain what he witnessed
- Refuses to look Hollow in the eye afterward
- Required to attend Faction questioning — that's the only reason
  he and Hollow are ever in the same room again
- His official report just says "civilian assisted"
- Running gag: every time something insane happens around Hollow,
  cut to Darius's face. Just his face. No words needed.

---

### HZ Rule 8: Awakening Connection
Hollow's awakening and his sensei's death are connected.
The same incursion that killed his sensei exposed Hollow
to Void energy and triggered his awakening.
It also made his grandma sick.
Hollow does not know any of this yet.
This is revealed gradually across multiple chapters.

## SECTION 8 — PRE-GENERATION CHECKLIST

Before generating ANY story content run this checklist:

**WORLD:**
- [ ] Can the world be explained in 3 panels?
- [ ] Is there a civilian reaction shot to establish normalcy?
- [ ] Is there action before character introduction?
- [ ] Are systems explained before stakes depend on them?

**CHARACTER:**
- [ ] Is the introduction 2 sentences or less?
- [ ] Are the stakes visible not narrated?
- [ ] Is the weakness shown before the power?
- [ ] Is there ONE clear want?
- [ ] Is every character action motivated by self interest or survival?

**SCENE:**
- [ ] Does every scene have one job?
- [ ] Does every scene pass the 4-question logic test?
- [ ] Does comedy come from character not randomness?
- [ ] Is the timeline realistic?
- [ ] Has the reader been hooked before any slice of life appears?

**PACING:**
- [ ] Does text follow the volume rules?
- [ ] Are 20% of panels silent?
- [ ] Does the chapter follow the opening and closing formulas?

**INFORMATION:**
- [ ] Is only necessary information revealed now?
- [ ] Is everything else saved for later?
- [ ] Does the chapter end on a hook not an answer?

**LOGIC:**
- [ ] Does every character action have a selfish or survival motivation?
- [ ] Does every location make sense for these characters?
- [ ] Does every joke come from character AND world logic?
- [ ] Does the timeline hold up to scrutiny?
- [ ] Can every character's presence be justified in one sentence? (Rule 23)

## VOID MECHANICS

- Every Void Hole leads to a different interior environment — random every time
- Environments can be: ancient ruins, caves, forests, tundra, ocean floors,
  urban decay, deserts, floating islands — anything
- Monsters inside match the environment type
- No two Void runs are identical — Anoms can never fully prepare
- This is why higher ranked Anoms are valuable — they adapt
- Hollow surviving is more impressive because conditions are always unknown
- The randomness also means the Factions can never fully map or predict Voids
- Some Voids are small and simple. Some are massive labyrinths.
- Void difficulty does not always match the Void Hole's external rank classification
  — a Class-D Void Hole can sometimes contain Class-B interior conditions
  — this is rare but happens. It's called a Void Surge.

---

## AWAKENING MECHANICS

- Happens when a person is exposed to concentrated Void energy and survives
- 1 in 10,000 people exposed actually awaken
- Most others die or get Void Sickness
- No warning signs — person collapses, wakes up different
- Ability doesn't announce itself — person notices things are different
- Factions detect awakened people using scanners
- Hollow awakened during the incursion that killed his sensei
- Same incursion caused grandma's Void Sickness
- Hollow does not know these events are connected

## FACTION ECONOMY

- Void Holes contain creatures AND valuable resources/artifacts
- Factions are funded by governments to manage Void Holes
- Factions assign contracts to Anoms — salaried or per job
- Freelance Anoms need Faction approval to run any Void
- Running a Void without approval = illegal
- E-rank Anoms make almost nothing
- Higher ranks = better contracts = more money
- Medical treatment for Void Sickness is expensive
- Factions control access to the best treatments
